Three Approaches to Autonomous Translation

Same vision, different architectures for how the AI pipeline and human review interact

A

The Assembly Line

Metaphor: Factory with quality checkpoints

Books flow through a linear pipeline: Import → AI Translate → Confidence Score → Auto-approve (high confidence) → Flag for review (low confidence) → Human review → Publish

The AI translates chapter by chapter. Each segment gets a confidence score. High-confidence segments are auto-approved. Low-confidence segments land in a review queue. Reviewers work through the queue, not through whole books.

Pros: Maximum throughput. Reviewers only see what needs attention. Easy to measure (% auto-approved, queue depth).
Cons: Loses the "reading experience" — reviewers see fragments, not flowing prose. Risk of coherence issues across auto-approved segments that were individually fine but collectively drift.

B

The Editor's Desk

Metaphor: Publishing house manuscript review

The AI produces complete book drafts. A senior reviewer reads the translation as a reader — flowing text, not segments. AI annotations appear as margin notes: uncertain passages highlighted, alternative renderings offered, doctrinal flags raised.

The reviewer reads, marks issues at a high level ("this section feels wrong", "rephrase this concept"), and the AI revises. Multiple pass cycle until the reviewer approves.

Pros: Preserves reading flow. Catches coherence issues. Natural for senior reviewers. Feels like editing a manuscript, not grading an exam.
Cons: Slower per-book — reviewer reads everything even if 90% is fine. Harder to parallelize across the team.

C

The Hybrid — Tiered Review

Metaphor: Assembly line feeds the editor's desk

Combines A and B with a two-tier system:

Tier 1 (automated): AI translates entire books. Confidence scoring auto-approves routine passages. Flags exceptions into a fast review queue for quick fixes.

Tier 2 (manuscript review): After Tier 1 clears, a senior reviewer reads the complete draft — but now with 90% already validated, they're reading for flow, voice, and doctrinal coherence rather than catching basic errors.

The reviewer's corrections at both tiers feed back into the AI's knowledge base.

Pros: Best of both worlds — speed of the pipeline plus coherence of manuscript review. Tier 1 handles volume, Tier 2 ensures quality. Feedback loops from both tiers.
Cons: Most complex to build. Two distinct interfaces needed. Requires clear criteria for what Tier 1 auto-approves vs. flags.

My Recommendation: C — The Hybrid

For 300+ books across multiple languages, you need the throughput of the assembly line. But for Buddhist texts where doctrinal precision and voice matter, you also need the manuscript-level coherence check. The hybrid gives you both — and the two tiers naturally map to different team roles (junior reviewers on Tier 1 queue, senior reviewers on Tier 2 manuscripts).
